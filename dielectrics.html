What is Dielectrics?

A dielectric is an insulating material that becomes polarized in an electric field, meaning its molecules shift slightly without conducting current.

  • It reduces the net electric field between capacitor plates and increases charge storage for a given voltage.

  • Its key property is permittivity (ε), especially relative permittivity (εᵣ), which measures how much the material reduces the field compared to a vacuum.

Core Concept

The main principle is polarization: external electric fields cause molecules in the dielectric to form tiny dipoles aligned with the field.

These dipoles create an internal field opposing the external one, thus weakening the overall field inside the dielectric.

This effect increases the capacitance of a capacitor by a factor of εᵣ. Materials like water, glass, and ceramics have high εᵣ values.

Michael Faraday (1791–1867)

Michael Faraday introduced the concept of dielectric materials in the 1830s. He discovered that inserting an insulating material between the plates of a capacitor altered its ability to store charge. His experiments led to the identification of the dielectric constant, a critical property still used to describe how materials respond to electric fields.

James Clerk Maxwell (1831–1879)

James Clerk Maxwell incorporated dielectric behavior into his foundational equations of electromagnetism. He introduced the concept of electric displacement, which separates the effect of free charges from the polarization of the dielectric. His work enabled a unified theory of electricity, magnetism, and light.

Hermann von Helmholtz (1821–1894)

Hermann von Helmholtz studied the dynamic response of dielectrics to changing electric fields. He investigated dielectric relaxation and energy dissipation, paving the way for understanding how real materials behave in alternating fields, which is vital in AC circuit design and high-frequency applications.

Sir Nevill Mott (1905–1996)

Sir Nevill Mott contributed to the quantum mechanical understanding of dielectric materials. His work in solid-state physics clarified how electrons move in insulators and semiconductors, helping to explain dielectric breakdown and conductivity at the atomic level. His research remains influential in modern materials science and electronics.
